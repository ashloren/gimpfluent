Learn How to use GIMP for all Your Photo Editing & Graphics Needs

Toolbox & Layers in GIMP

First and foremost, every time you open an image or create a new one using GIMP, you will want to make sure you have both your Toolbox and Layers dialogs open at all times.

If these do not appear by default, go the the toolbar and click on the Windows option, then scroll down to Dockable Dialogs, then choose Layers.

Repeat this process and choose New Toolbox to make both windows appear.

Selection Tools in GIMP

There are several tools available in GIMP to make selections of a photo or image.

The main two we will be dealing with in this guide are the Rectangle Select tool and the Ellipse Select tool.

As the name suggests, the Rectangle select tool allows you to click anywhere on a photo or image and drag your cursor in order to make a rectangular selection of the image.

The Ellipse Selection tool allows you to make circular selections of images instead.

There are several useful actions you can use in GIMP once you've made a selection using either of these tools.

For example, you can hit the Delete key on your keyboard and the selection you made will be removed from the image. This is similar to how a cut and paste function works in many other softwares.

Another option would be to use your Bucket Fill tool to fill the selection with a color of your choice.

Image Guides in GIMP

A useful trick I use to ensure that my edits stay within the dimensions of a given photo is to open in GIMP, then Select All.

Next, go to Image ---> Guides ---> New Guides From Selection

This will place guide lines around the perimeter of the photo you are editing, making it easy to keep your changes within your workspace.

Another trick you may find useful is to also add horizontal and vertical guides at your halfway points.

To do this, go to Image ---> Guides ---> New Guide (by Percent)

When the window pops up, choose 50% and horizontal. Then repeat these steps, changing horizontal to vertical the second time.

This is especially handy if you are creating graphics or drawing, as you can keep the middle points of your workspace in mind as your creation develops.
